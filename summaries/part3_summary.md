# Part 3 Summary — Organizational Authenticity Index

For Part 3, I built an organizational authenticity index by comparing the stated-values text from Part 1 with the lived-values proxy statement text from Part 2. I define authenticity here as language alignment: the extent to which what a company says it values publicly is reflected in what it emphasizes in formal corporate disclosures.

The index has three components. First, I used theme overlap to measure whether the same broad value categories appeared in both the stated-values page and the proxy statement. Second, I used theme-emphasis similarity to compare whether the two documents emphasized those categories in similar proportions. Third, I used tone alignment as a smaller supporting signal to compare whether the overall tone direction was similar across the two texts.

The final index uses a weighted average: 40% theme overlap, 45% theme-emphasis similarity, and 15% tone alignment. I gave the largest weight to theme-emphasis similarity because simply mentioning the same values is not enough; the relative emphasis also matters. Theme overlap still receives substantial weight because shared categories are the clearest basic sign of alignment. Tone alignment receives a smaller weight because it is useful, but more limited than the theme-based measures.

The final dataset keeps all 450 company-year rows. The authenticity index could be calculated for 373 rows, or 82.9% of the dataset. The remaining 77 rows were labeled as `insufficient_data` because either the stated-values page or the proxy statement did not have usable text. Among scored rows, the average authenticity index was 0.579 and the median was 0.595. Scores ranged from 0.111 to 0.9055, which gives the measure enough spread to compare companies and sectors.

Most scored rows fell into the moderate-alignment group. The final label counts were 230 moderate-alignment rows, 102 low-alignment rows, 41 high-alignment rows, and 77 insufficient-data rows. This distribution seems reasonable because most companies use at least some overlapping values language across public-facing pages and proxy statements, but relatively few companies show very high measured alignment.

The sector summaries show meaningful variation. Energy had the highest average authenticity score, followed by Financials and Healthcare. Consumer Discretionary and Technology scored lower on average. This does not necessarily mean one sector is more authentic in a behavioral sense, but it suggests that some sectors have closer alignment between stated-values language and proxy disclosure language under this measurement approach.

As a basic validity check, I compared the top and bottom companies by average authenticity index. Higher-scoring firms generally showed stronger theme overlap and stronger theme-emphasis similarity between their stated-values pages and proxy statements. Lower-scoring firms generally had weaker overlap or weaker emphasis similarity. This is a useful sanity check because the index behaves in the expected direction: companies with more similar stated and disclosed values language tend to score higher.

There are several limitations. First, the index compares two very different document types. Stated-values pages are public-facing and often aspirational, while proxy statements are formal governance disclosures. Second, the measure relies on broad dictionary-based categories, which makes it transparent but can miss more subtle language. Third, the tone component is a rough dictionary-based score, not a full sentiment model. Finally, the index measures language alignment, not actual corporate behavior. I interpret it as a structured proxy for authenticity, not as proof that a company truly lives its values.
