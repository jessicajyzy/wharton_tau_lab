# Part 4 Summary — Exploring Types of Authenticity Gaps

For Part 4, I wanted to look beyond the overall authenticity score and ask what kind of mismatch drives lower alignment. A low score can mean different things. In some cases, a company’s stated-values page and proxy statement may talk about mostly different value categories. In other cases, they may mention similar categories but emphasize them very differently. These are not the same pattern, so I created a gap-type analysis to separate them.

I classified each company-year using the component scores from Part 3. Rows with strong theme overlap and strong theme-emphasis similarity were labeled as high alignment. Rows with strong theme overlap but weaker emphasis similarity were labeled as same themes, different emphasis. Rows with weaker theme overlap but similar tone were labeled as different themes, similar tone. Rows with weak theme overlap and weak emphasis similarity were labeled as broad misalignment. Rows without enough text from both sources were kept as insufficient data.

I also added a component-level diagnostic to identify what was dragging the score down. For each scored company-year, I looked at the weakest component among theme overlap, theme-emphasis similarity, and tone alignment. This made the extension more useful because it does not only assign a gap type; it also points to the part of the index that is contributing most to the gap.

The most common scored gap type was same themes, different emphasis. It appeared in 162 company-year rows, or 43.4% of scored rows. This was the most interesting finding from the extension. It suggests that many companies are not necessarily saying one thing publicly and something completely different in proxy statements. More often, they mention similar broad value categories, but the weight placed on those categories changes across document types.

The component-level results support this interpretation. The most common gap driver was theme-emphasis gap, which appeared in 259 rows, or 69.4% of scored rows. Tone gap appeared in 60 rows, and theme-overlap gap appeared in 54 rows. In other words, the main source of misalignment was usually not that companies had no shared values language at all. It was that the relative emphasis of those values shifted between public-facing stated-values pages and formal proxy disclosures.

The distribution of gap types also gives useful context. High alignment appeared in 86 rows, or 23.1% of scored rows. Different themes with similar tone appeared in 70 rows, or 18.8% of scored rows. Broad misalignment appeared in 55 rows, or 14.7% of scored rows. This suggests that complete mismatch is not the dominant pattern. The more common issue is subtler: companies often share values vocabulary across documents, but they do not always prioritize the same values.

The sector breakdown makes the extension more useful as a diagnostic tool. For example, Financials had the highest share of high-alignment rows, while Healthcare had a large share of same-themes-different-emphasis rows. This does not prove that one sector is more authentic than another, but it suggests that different sectors may produce different types of alignment gaps.

I do not treat these categories as final labels. They are exploratory and depend on thresholds chosen from the Part 3 component scores. Still, this analysis makes the authenticity index more interpretable. Instead of only saying whether a company-year scores high or low, it helps explain what kind of mismatch the score is detecting.

The main limitation is that this analysis inherits the limitations of the authenticity index itself. The theme categories are broad and dictionary-based, the two document types have different purposes, and some company-years do not have enough usable text. Even so, the extension gives a more useful view of alignment by showing not only whether companies appear aligned, but how their alignment breaks down.
