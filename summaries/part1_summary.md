# Part 1 Summary — Stated Values from Archived Corporate Pages

For Part 1, I collected archived stated-values pages for 50 large S&P 500 companies across five sectors from 2016 through 2024. The goal was to capture what companies publicly said they valued over time, using corporate About Us, mission, values, purpose, responsibility, sustainability, or equivalent pages from the Wayback Machine.

The final dataset contains 450 company-year rows. Usable text was extracted for 85.6% of rows, or 385 out of 450 company-years. Coverage was reasonably balanced across years, ranging from 80% to 90%. Sector coverage was strongest in Energy and Financials, and somewhat lower in Technology and Consumer Discretionary. The lower coverage in some sectors was mainly due to companies whose archived pages were missing, JavaScript-heavy, blocked, server-error pages, or did not preserve readable body text in Wayback captures.

To identify the correct page, I used a rule-based page selection process. The scraper first searched the company’s seed About URL, then tried curated backup URLs, historical URL variants, prefix matches, and limited domain-level fallback. Candidate URLs were scored based on whether they contained terms such as “about,” “company,” “who-we-are,” “mission,” “purpose,” “values,” “responsibility,” “citizenship,” or “sustainability.” Pages related to shopping, products, support, login/account, investor-only content, PDFs, images, search pages, sitemaps, and other irrelevant content were excluded. This approach was designed to balance completeness with robustness.

For text extraction, the scraper removed scripts, styles, navigation, headers, footers, and common boilerplate. It used a minimum usable-text threshold of 400 characters. Rows selected through weaker fallback methods, rows with lower page scores, and rows with shorter text were marked with a `needs_review` flag. Rows that did not produce usable text were retained in the dataset and explained in `analyst_notes` instead of being silently dropped.

I defined nine value categories: innovation, customer focus, employee culture, diversity and inclusion, sustainability and environment, community and social impact, integrity/ethics/trust, health/safety/wellbeing, and financial performance/growth. These categories were chosen because they capture common forms of corporate values language and can later be compared with disclosure language in Part 2.

I also added an LLM-assisted analysis layer for the 385 usable stated-values snapshots. For each usable company-year page, the LLM reviewed the cleaned page text and returned structured fields for value categories, whether the page appeared materially changed from the prior usable year, a short summary of the main values emphasized, and any notable linguistic shift. The final merged dataset preserves all 450 company-year rows; LLM fields are populated for usable rows and left blank for the 65 rows without usable text.

The scraper also compares each usable company-year page to the previous usable year for the same company. This creates a `changed_from_prior` indicator and a text similarity score. This comparison is useful for identifying whether a company’s stated-values language stayed stable over time or changed meaningfully. The LLM layer adds a more qualitative interpretation of these shifts, while the rule-based similarity score keeps the comparison reproducible.

The main limitation is that Wayback coverage is uneven. Some companies changed domains or page structures over time, and some archived pages existed but returned empty, blocked, JavaScript-heavy, or server-error captures. Rather than forcing lower-quality pages into the dataset, I retained these rows as documented gaps. The theme-coding approach is also limited because predefined dictionaries may miss more subtle values language, while the LLM layer depends on the quality of the extracted text.

The Part 1 dataset is strong enough for downstream analysis because it has broad coverage, transparent selection rules, a raw Wayback HTML archive, review flags, explicit gap documentation, and an LLM-assisted enrichment layer for all usable scraped snapshots.
